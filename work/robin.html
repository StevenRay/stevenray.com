The Challenge

Lawn care is a $130B industry that hadn't changed in decades. Homeowners were stuck choosing between expensive full-service companies or spending weekends pushing gas-powered mowers. Meanwhile, autonomous technology was maturing rapidly in every adjacent category, except the backyard.

The opportunity was clear: bring robotics to lawn care the way Roomba brought it to vacuuming. But the challenge wasn't just building the technology. It was creating a service model that made autonomous mowing accessible to everyday homeowners.

The Approach

Robin started inside Dialexa Labs as a venture that combined our hardware engineering capabilities with a consumer service model. We took a phased approach to the market.

  • Launched initially as an on-demand lawn care marketplace to build the customer base and understand the market
  • Introduced autonomous robotic mowers as a Robots-as-a-Service offering at roughly $17/week, no equipment to buy
  • Built the software platform for fleet management, scheduling, and remote monitoring of robotic mower deployments
  • Scaled through a franchise model, opening 15 locations to prove the economics in diverse markets

Robin appeared on Shark Tank, generating massive consumer interest: 1,300 clicks per second during the episode and over 700 franchise leads. The company grew to 1,000+ customers across 15 franchise locations with backing from Husqvarna Group and MTD Products.

In 2019, Fahey Group acquired Robin, consolidating it with Mowbot to create Robin Technologies, now operating a Robots-as-a-Service platform for landscapers nationwide, including a pilot at DFW International Airport's 14,000+ acres.

What I Learned

Robin proved that the venture studio model could work in physical products, not just software. Incubating hardware ventures requires a different kind of patience: longer development cycles, supply chain complexity, and the unforgiving physics of real-world deployment.

The biggest lesson was about market timing and wedge strategies. We started with a service marketplace because autonomous mowers weren't ready for prime time yet. That gave us the customer relationships and market knowledge to introduce robotics when the technology caught up. Sometimes the best way to build the future is to start with what works today.
